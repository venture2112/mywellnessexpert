My Story: From Frustrated Doctor to Functional Wellness Pioneer

Thirty years ago, I started my chiropractic practice with the same enthusiasm and idealism that most young doctors have. I wanted to help people. I wanted to heal.

But something bothered me. I noticed that while my adjustments helped patients feel better temporarily, many of them kept coming back with the same issues. The pain would return. The problems would persist.

Then came the turning point.

A patient named Margaret came to see me. She was 62 years old and had been suffering from chronic back pain for over a decade. She'd seen specialists, taken medications, even considered surgery. Nothing worked.

Instead of just adjusting her spine and sending her on her way, I decided to dig deeper. I asked about her diet, her sleep, her stress levels, her daily movement patterns. I ran comprehensive tests that went beyond standard medical workups.

What I discovered changed everything.

Margaret's "back pain" wasn't just a spinal issue. It was the result of systemic inflammation caused by food sensitivities she didn't know she had, combined with poor posture from sitting at a desk for 40 years, and stress that was literally making her muscles tight and painful.

When we addressed ALL of these factors—not just her spine—something remarkable happened. Within three months, Margaret's pain was 80% better. Within six months, she told me she felt better than she had in 20 years.

The Problem With Modern Healthcare

Here's what I learned from Margaret and thousands of patients like her: The traditional medical model is broken when it comes to chronic health issues.

Most doctors are trained to:

  • Diagnose symptoms and prescribe medications to mask them
  • Specialize in one body part while ignoring the whole system
  • Spend an average of 7 minutes with each patient (not enough time to understand complex issues)
  • Treat acute problems effectively but struggle with chronic conditions
The Result? Millions of people are stuck in a cycle of temporary relief followed by recurring symptoms. They're told to "learn to live with it" or that their problems are "just part of getting older."
